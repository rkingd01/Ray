Empowerment of Russian Peasantry

Source: Historian Orlando Figes, Birkbeck College, London Univ.

The protestors are well organised and carry banners which read 'We kolkhoz workers call for the liquidation of the Kulaks as a class'.

In keeping with Stalinist ideology the state encouraged peasants through the promise of empowerment. Stalin succeeded in 'removing' the affluent class by giving unlimited powers to the Cheka (NKVD) terror network. An unrelenting stream of state-controlled propaganda targeted the peasant class constantly reminding them how affluent class had prospered while they starved.

Stalin reasoned that this class was responsible for the failure of the cooperatives and therefore culpable for the shortages that followed.
There were of course other factors at play:


  • Private farmers had been less than supportive of the Oktober Revolution and it was feared that any counter-revolution would come from this source.
  • In Ingria many successful farmers and their families were of a Finnic origin and given the undisguised policy of ethnic cleansing were prime targets.
  • The land seized from Finnic farmers greatly expanded collective farms.
  • Powerful imagery demonised the landowners whilst amplifying the demise of the peasant class. Many of these landowners were executed and many more were deported.
  • Despite the atrocities committed the Cheka secret police were untouchable. Indeed, the horrific details of their savagery was part of their modus operandi.
